Summer 2017 - Sift Science

Software Engineering Intern @ San Francisco, CA



Sift Science is a Y-Combinator alum and a high growth start-up specializing in machine learning fraud protection. At Sift, I worked on a variety of projects. Firstly, I implemented ~100 new features that helped improve our machine learning models. I also used MapReduce and Apache Crunch to cut down on feature extraction and model training times by over 90%.

My main project was to implement Naive Bayes text classification models. Although it was incredibly fun to write a Machine Learning model from scratch, Naive Bayes models are notoriously known for their simplicity. Thus, the real challenge was how to scale these models to handle 150 million transactions a day and over 12 TB of training data. Via distributed system frameworks and some clever programming, I was able to make these models very memory efficient and also very fast to train and generate predictions. It was extremely rewarding to have the opportunity to write a scalable Machine Learning system from scratch to finish, and also realize that I am helping protect billions of people from fraud every week.

Winter 2016 - Cipher Systems

Software Development Intern @ Annapolis, MD



At Cipher Systems, I was initially introduced to the .NET framework. Although I am still far from an expert, I found that my time at Cipher Systems taught me about a new paradigm of programming that is extremely widespread in industry.

At Cipher Systems, my main project was to provide some way to semantically tag news articles. Previously, Cipher relied on IBM Watson to do this, and that service was incredibly expensive. Using Stanford CoreNLP's natural language processing library, I wrote a NLP pipeline in Java that could replicate IBM Watson's role, thus saving the company quite a lot of money. This pipeline was eventually transformed into a Java microservice, and finally translated into C#.



Fall 2016 - Content Analytics

Software Engineering Intern @ San Francisco, CA



At ContentAnalytics, my main task was to help companies verify their inventories across all third-party retailer websites. Namely, these companies wanted to make sure that the pictures and information the third-party sellers had on their products matched their expectations. To tackle this, I wrote a Python script that utilizes a fleet of webcrawlers to perform image comparisons on thousands of images. I found the problem's lack of a closed form solution very fun, as it meant that I had to be creative and innovative in my approach.



Summer 2016 - University of Maryland, Department of Computer Science

Undergraduate Research Fellow @ College Park,



I investigated Pollard-Rho's semi-prime factoring algorithm. Factoring algorithms are incredibly important as the hardness of the problem is the basis for almost all cryptographic schemes. My team and I were able to cut down Pollard-Rho's runtime by a constant amount of about 97% and our version of his algorithm is potentially one of the fastest factoring algorithms for numbers less than 10^7.
